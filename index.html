Timeline of Discovery

The modern understanding of the atom was built over two centuries through key experiments and theoretical breakthroughs. This timeline highlights the major milestones that shaped our view of the atom's structure, from an indivisible sphere to a complex quantum system. Click on each event to learn more about its significance.

1808 - John Dalton

Dalton's Atomic Theory

Matter is composed of indivisible atoms; atoms of a given element are identical.

1897 - J.J. Thomson

Discovery of the Electron

Using a cathode ray tube, Thomson discovered the electron, a negatively charged subatomic particle, leading to his "plum pudding" model of the atom.

1911 - Ernest Rutherford

Discovery of the Nucleus

Through his gold foil experiment, Rutherford concluded that the atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus with electrons orbiting it.

1913 - Niels Bohr

Bohr's Atomic Model

Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed, quantized orbits around the nucleus, explaining the line spectrum of hydrogen.

1926 - Erwin Schrödinger

Quantum Mechanical Model

Schrödinger developed a wave equation that describes electrons as waves, leading to the concept of orbitals as regions of probability.

Core Concepts

Before diving into atomic models, it's crucial to understand the terminology used to classify different atoms and ions based on their subatomic particles. This section defines these fundamental classifications.

Isotopes

Atoms with the same number of protons (same Z) but a different number of neutrons (different A).

Example: Protium (¹H), Deuterium (²H), Tritium (³H)

Isobars

Atoms of different elements with the same mass number (A) but different atomic numbers (Z).

Example: ¹⁴C and ¹⁴N

Isotones

Atoms of different elements with the same number of neutrons (A-Z).

Example: ¹³C (7n) and ¹⁴N (7n)

Isoelectronic Species

Atoms or ions that have the same number of electrons.

Example: N³⁻, O²⁻, Ne, Na⁺ (all have 10 electrons)

Atomic Models

Our model of the atom has evolved significantly over time. Each model built upon the last, correcting its flaws and adding new layers of understanding. This section allows you to compare the key atomic models side-by-side.

Thomson's "Plum Pudding" Model (1897)

Proposed the atom was a uniform sphere of positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded within it, much like plums in a pudding.

Key Limitations:

  • Could not explain the results of Rutherford's gold foil experiment.
  • Did not account for the neutrality of the atom in a stable way.

Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

Based on his α-particle scattering experiment, Rutherford proposed a model where the atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center, with electrons orbiting it.

Key Limitations:

  • Violated classical physics: An orbiting electron should continuously lose energy and spiral into the nucleus.
  • Could not explain the discrete line spectra of atoms.

Bohr's Planetary Model (1913)

Bohr modified Rutherford's model by proposing that electrons exist in specific, quantized orbits with fixed energy levels. Electrons only absorb or emit energy when they jump between these orbits.

Key Limitations:

  • Only worked perfectly for single-electron species (like H, He⁺).
  • Could not explain the Zeeman effect or Stark effect (splitting of spectral lines).
  • Violated the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle.

Quantum Mechanical Model (1926)

The modern model treats electrons as waves, based on work by de Broglie and Schrödinger. It does not define an exact path for an electron but describes a region of probability called an orbital where the electron is likely to be found.

Key Features:

  • Based on probability, not certainty.
  • Introduced the concept of quantum numbers to describe an electron's state.
  • Successfully explains the behavior of multi-electron atoms.

Bohr's Model & Hydrogen Spectrum

Quantum Mechanical Model

The modern model of the atom is based on quantum mechanics, which describes electrons not as particles in fixed orbits, but as waves in 3D regions of probability called orbitals. An electron's state is fully described by a set of four unique quantum numbers.

Quantum Number Rules

Principal (n): Shell/Energy level. Values: 1, 2, 3, ...

Azimuthal (l): Subshell/Shape. Values: 0 to (n-1).

Magnetic (mₗ): Orbital Orientation. Values: -l to +l.

Spin (mₛ): Electron Spin. Values: +1/2 or -1/2.

Nodes

A node is a region with zero electron probability.

Total Nodes = n - 1

Angular Nodes = l

Radial Nodes = n - l - 1

Electronic Configuration

The distribution of electrons among the various atomic orbitals is called the electronic configuration. It is governed by a set of rules that determine how electrons occupy the available energy levels to achieve the most stable arrangement.

Aufbau Principle

Electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals available first. Energy is determined by the (n+l) rule.

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. An orbital can hold a max of two electrons with opposite spins.

Hund's Rule

For degenerate orbitals, electrons fill each orbital singly before any orbital gets a second electron.
